# The Ultimate Guide on How to Fix a Tap (and Reclaim Your Sanity)

That sound. Drip. Drip. Drip. It starts as a minor annoyance, a background noise you can easily ignore. But soon, it becomes the only thing you can hear. It’s the soundtrack to your late-night thoughts and the first thing you notice in the morning. A dripping tap isn't just a test of your patience; it's a constant reminder of wasted water and money going, quite literally, down the drain. According to the [US Environmental Protection Agency](https://www.epa.gov/watersense/fix-leak-week), a single leaky faucet can waste thousands of gallons of water per year. The good news? You don't have to live with it, and you probably don't need to spend a fortune on a plumber. Learning **how to fix a tap** is one of the most rewarding DIY skills you can master. It's a project that seems intimidating at first, but with a little guidance and the right tools, it's surprisingly straightforward. This guide is designed to walk you through the entire process, from figuring out what tools you need to identifying your tap type and performing the repair. We'll break it all down into simple, manageable steps, so you can silence that drip for good, save some money, and earn some serious DIY bragging rights. Get ready to empower yourself and bring peace and quiet back to your home.

## Gear Up: The Essential Toolkit for Fixing a Leaky Tap

Before you dive in and start taking things apart, a little preparation goes a long way. Having the right tools on hand will make the job smoother, faster, and much less frustrating. You don’t need a professional-grade plumbing setup; most of the items required are likely already in your toolbox or are inexpensive to pick up from a local hardware store. Think of this as your tap-fixing mission kit. First and foremost, you'll need an adjustable spanner (also called a crescent wrench). This is your most important tool, as it can be adjusted to fit various nut sizes on the tap. To avoid scratching the chrome finish of your tap, it’s a great idea to wrap the jaws of the spanner with electrical tape or use a thick cloth between the tool and the tap. Next, gather a set of screwdrivers, including both a flat-head and a Phillips-head. These are usually needed to remove the handle of the tap. A pair of pliers can also be useful for gripping stubborn parts, but use them gently to avoid causing damage.

Of course, you'll also need the replacement part itself. For traditional taps, this is usually a set of rubber washers and O-rings. For modern taps, it might be a complete ceramic or plastic cartridge. It's a good idea to buy a multi-pack of assorted washers, as they are very cheap and it saves you a return trip to the store if you pick the wrong size. When it comes to the actual prep work for the job, the most critical step is to shut off the water supply to the tap you're working on. Look for small valves, called isolator valves, on the pipes directly underneath the sink. You can usually turn these a quarter-turn with a flat-head screwdriver to shut off the water. If you can't find them, you'll need to turn off your home's main water supply at the stopcock. Once the water is off, turn the tap on to drain any remaining water from the pipe. Finally, place an old towel in the bottom of the sink to protect the surface from any dropped tools, and be sure to put the plug in the drain. This one simple step has saved countless DIYers from watching a tiny, essential screw disappear forever. This preparation is the foundation of knowing **how to fix a tap** successfully.

## Know Your Tap: A Guide to Common Household Taps

So, you’ve got your tools and you’ve shut off the water. The next crucial step in figuring out **how to fix a tap** is identifying what kind of tap you actually have. The repair method varies significantly depending on the internal mechanism, so a little detective work now will save you a lot of guesswork later. Most household taps fall into one of a few main categories, and you can usually tell which one you have just by looking at it and how it operates. The most common type, especially in older homes, is the compression tap, also known as a washer tap. These are the classic taps with two separate handles for hot and cold that you have to turn several times to get the water flowing at full blast. The mechanism is simple: turning the handle raises or lowers a stem, which has a rubber washer on the end that presses against a valve seat to stop the water flow. In ninety-nine percent of cases, a leak in a compression tap is caused by a worn-out, degraded washer that no longer creates a perfect seal. This is often the easiest type of tap to fix.

In more modern homes, you're likely to find ceramic disc taps. These can have two handles or a single lever that controls both temperature and flow. Instead of a rubber washer, they contain two highly polished ceramic discs that slide over each other. One disc is fixed, while the other moves with the handle. When the holes in the discs align, water flows through; when they are out of alignment, the water is blocked. These taps feel very smooth to operate and only require a quarter or half turn to go from off to fully on. When they leak, it's usually because the neoprene seals around the cartridge have worn out or one of the discs has a hairline crack. For these, you won't be replacing a washer, but the entire ceramic cartridge unit. The key is to get an exact replacement, so taking the old one with you to the hardware store is essential. Other common types include ball taps and cartridge taps, which are often single-lever kitchen taps. They use a combination of O-rings, seals, and a central mechanism (either a hollow ball or a cartridge) to control the water. Like ceramic disc taps, the fix usually involves replacing the entire internal cartridge.

## Step-by-Step Guide: How to Fix a Tap with a Washer

If you’ve identified your dripping culprit as a traditional compression tap, you’re in luck. This is the most common type of repair and is a fantastic entry point into the world of DIY plumbing. The process is very logical and requires just a few basic tools. By following these steps carefully, you’ll master the fundamentals of **how to fix a tap** in no time. Remember, the water supply should be turned off and the sink drain should be plugged before you begin.

1.  **Remove the Handle:** Most tap handles are topped with a decorative cap, which might indicate "Hot" or "Cold." You can usually pry this cap off gently with a small flat-head screwdriver to reveal a screw underneath. Unscrew this retaining screw and the handle should lift straight off. If it’s stuck, a little gentle wiggling back and forth should loosen it.
2.  **Expose the Valve:** With the handle removed, you’ll see the valve mechanism. There will be a large hexagonal nut, often called the bonnet or valve cover, holding it in place. Using your adjustable spanner (with a cloth to protect the finish), turn this nut counter-clockwise to loosen it and remove it completely.
3.  **Lift Out the Valve Assembly:** Once the bonnet is off, you can lift out the entire inner valve assembly, sometimes called the headgear or jumper. This is the component that holds the all-important washer.
4.  **Replace the Washer:** At the very bottom of the valve assembly, you will see the rubber washer. It’s usually held in place by a small screw or it might just be pressed on. Use a screwdriver to remove it. It will likely look compressed, cracked, or worn down. This little piece is the source of your leak. Take your new washer, making sure it’s the correct size, and screw or press it into place.
5.  **Clean and Inspect:** Before you reassemble, take a moment to clean any gunk or mineral buildup (limescale) from the valve parts and from inside the main body of the tap. Feel inside the tap body with your finger; the metal rim that the washer presses against is called the valve seat. If it feels rough or damaged, it could be the cause of the leak, but usually, a new washer is all that's needed.
6.  **Reassemble and Test:** Now, simply do everything in reverse. Drop the valve assembly back in, hand-tighten the bonnet nut, and then give it a final snug turn with the spanner (don't overtighten it). Place the handle back on, tighten the screw, and pop the decorative cap back on. Turn your water supply back on slowly and check for any leaks. Finally, turn the tap on and off a few times. Congratulations, you’ve just learned how to fix a dripping tap!

## Fixing Modern Taps: A Guide to Ceramic Disc and Cartridge Repairs

While the classic washer tap is a straightforward fix, don't be discouraged if you have a more modern ceramic disc or cartridge tap. The process of learning **how to fix a tap** of this style is just as manageable, though the replacement part is different. Instead of swapping out a tiny rubber washer, you’ll typically be replacing a single, self-contained unit known as a cartridge. This often makes the repair even quicker, although the replacement part is a bit more expensive than a simple washer. The most important rule with these taps is ensuring you buy the correct replacement cartridge. There are hundreds of different shapes and sizes, so your best bet is to take the old one with you to the store or take several clear photos of it from different angles.

The disassembly process for these modern taps starts out much the same as a compression tap. First, ensure the water is off and the drain is plugged. You’ll need to locate and remove the handle. On single-lever mixer taps, there’s often a small grub screw hidden somewhere on the handle, sometimes concealed by a small plastic plug. You may need a small flat-head screwdriver or an Allen key to loosen this screw. Once loosened, the lever handle should lift off, revealing the top of the cartridge. You'll likely see a large retaining nut holding the cartridge in place. It might be a decorative shroud or a simple brass nut. Use your adjustable spanner to carefully unscrew this nut counter-clockwise. With the nut removed, you can now lift the old cartridge straight up and out of the tap body. Pay close attention to how it's seated; there are often small notches or lugs on the bottom of the cartridge that align with holes in the tap body to ensure it's installed correctly.

Before installing the new cartridge, give the inside of the tap body a quick clean with a cloth to remove any debris. Now, take your new, identical cartridge and place it into the tap body, making sure to align the lugs on the bottom with the corresponding holes. It should sit flush and feel secure. Once it's in place, you can re-thread the retaining nut and tighten it (again, snug but not overly tight). Put the handle back on, tighten the grub screw, and replace the decorative plug. Now for the moment of truth: turn the water supply back on slowly and thoroughly check for any leaks around the base of the tap and the handle. Operate the tap to check for smooth movement and a clean shut-off. Just like that, you've successfully tackled a modern tap repair and expanded your knowledge of how to fix a tap.

## All Fixed Up: Knowing When Your DIY Job is Done (or When to Call a Plumber)

You’ve done it. The tools are put away, the water is back on, and the house is blissfully silent. There’s no more maddening drip, just the quiet satisfaction of a job well done. You’ve successfully learned **how to fix a tap**, saved yourself some money, and prevented a significant amount of water waste. It's a skill that will serve you well for years to come. Take a moment to appreciate your work and the newfound peace. To keep your taps in good working order for longer, try to get into the habit of turning them off gently. There’s no need to wrench them tight, especially with washer-based taps, as this just compresses and wears out the washer faster. A gentle turn is all that’s needed to create a seal. By following the steps in this guide, you are now well-equipped to handle the most common tap leaks you're likely to encounter in your home.

However, an important part of any DIY project is knowing your limitations and recognizing when it's time to call in a professional. If you’ve replaced the washer or cartridge and the tap is still dripping, there might be a more complex issue at play. The valve seat (the metal rim inside the tap that the washer presses against) could be scratched or corroded, which will prevent a perfect seal. While there are special tools to re-grind a valve seat, this is often a job best left to a plumber. Furthermore, if you encounter a part, like the bonnet nut or a handle, that is completely seized and won't budge even with reasonable force, it’s wise to stop. Applying too much force can risk breaking the tap or, even worse, the pipework it's connected to, turning a small drip into a major flood. If you feel at all out of your depth or if the problem seems more complicated than a simple part replacement, there's no shame in calling a certified professional. For peace of mind, you can look for qualified professionals through organizations like the [Chartered Institute of Plumbing and Heating Engineering (CIPHE)](https://www.ciphe.org.uk/consumer/find-a-plumber/). But for now, enjoy your drip-free home. You’ve earned it.
